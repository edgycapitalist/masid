1. **EU AI Act**: The European Union has implemented the AI Act, which categorizes AI systems by risk level and imposes requirements accordingly [1]. The framework consists of four levels of risk: minimal, limited, high, and unacceptable. High-risk AI systems, such as those used in critical infrastructure, healthcare, and law enforcement, face strict compliance requirements, including:
	* Conformity assessments
	* Human oversight
	* Transparency obligations
	* Data quality and governance requirements
2. **China's Generative AI Regulations**: China has introduced regulations on generative AI, requiring content labeling and algorithmic transparency [2]. The regulations aim to promote accountability and transparency in AI development and deployment, particularly in areas such as:
	* Deepfakes
	* Synthetic media
	* AI-generated content
3. **United States**: The US has implemented an executive-order approach to regulating AI, with a focus on safety testing [3]. This approach enables the US government to respond rapidly to emerging AI technologies, but may also lead to inconsistencies in regulation.
4. **United Kingdom**: The UK has adopted a sector-specific, principles-based framework for regulating AI [4]. This approach allows for tailored guidelines and standards to be developed for specific sectors, balancing regulatory oversight with industry flexibility.

### Key Provisions and Comparisons
The regulatory frameworks have the following key provisions:

* **Risk-Based Approach**: The EU AI Act adopts a risk-based approach, where high-risk AI systems are subject to stricter compliance requirements [1].
* **Content Labeling and Algorithmic Transparency**: China's regulations on generative AI require content labeling, indicating whether the content is AI-generated, and algorithmic transparency, providing insights into the decision-making processes of AI systems [2].
* **Transparency and Explainability**: Both the EU AI Act and China's regulations emphasize the importance of transparency and explainability in AI systems, ensuring that users understand how AI decisions are made and what data is used to make those decisions.
* **Safety Testing**: The US executive-order approach focuses on safety testing, emphasizing the importance of ensuring AI systems are safe and reliable [3].
* **Sector-Specific Framework**: The UK's sector-specific framework provides tailored guidance for industries, allowing for more flexible and adaptive regulation [4].

The differing approaches to AI regulation reflect distinct regulatory philosophies, with the EU and China emphasizing risk-based frameworks and accountability, while the US prioritizes flexibility and rapid response, and the UK balances oversight with industry flexibility.
