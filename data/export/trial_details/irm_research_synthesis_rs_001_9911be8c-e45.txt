The EU AI Act categorizes AI systems into four risk levels: unacceptable risk, high risk, medium risk, and low risk. High-risk AI systems, such as those used in healthcare and transportation, face strict compliance requirements, including conformity assessments, human oversight, and transparency obligations. This risk-based approach aims to balance innovation with regulatory oversight, ensuring that AI systems are developed and deployed in a way that prioritizes public safety and trust.
2. **China's Generative AI Regulations**
China has implemented regulations specifically targeting generative AI, requiring content labeling and algorithmic transparency. These regulations aim to promote accountability, transparency, and trust in AI-generated content, addressing concerns around misinformation, intellectual property, and social stability. By requiring content labeling, China's regulations enable users to make informed decisions about the content they engage with, while algorithmic transparency helps to build trust in AI decision-making processes.

The United States has adopted an executive-order approach to AI regulation, opting for a targeted strategy over comprehensive legislation. This approach prioritizes safety testing, indicating a focus on ensuring the safe development and deployment of AI systems.
