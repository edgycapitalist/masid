2. **Dataset size**: The required dataset size grows in proportion to the model size to achieve a certain level of loss (Kaplan et al., 2020).

Hoffmann et al. (2022) argued that most models are undertrained relative to their parameter count. This suggests that:
1. **Undertraining**: Many large language models are not trained for a sufficient number of steps, leading to suboptimal performance (Hoffmann et al., 2022).
2. **Increased training time**: To achieve better results, models may require longer training times, which can be resource-intensive (Hoffmann et al., 2022).

### Implications
The findings from these two sources have significant implications for training large language models:
1. **Scaling**: As models grow in size, they require larger datasets and more compute to achieve a certain level of loss (Kaplan et al., 2020).
2. **Training duration**: Models may need to be trained for longer periods to avoid undertraining and achieve optimal performance (Hoffmann et al., 2022).

### Conclusion
In conclusion, the scaling laws established by Kaplan et al. (2020) and the training requirements discussed by Hoffmann et al. (2022) provide valuable insights into the challenges of training large language models. By understanding these relationships and implications, researchers and practitioners can design more effective training strategies for large language models.
